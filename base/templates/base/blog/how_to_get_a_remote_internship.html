What remote internships are (and aren’t)

A remote internship is a structured learning role you complete online with a real team—expect standups, deliverables, feedback, and collaboration via Slack/Zoom/Docs/Git. It’s not a loose “volunteer” gig with no scope. Always look for a clear manager, goals, and outcomes you can put on your resume.

Pros & cons of remote internships

ProsCons
Apply globally; more role variety. Fewer casual mentorship moments than in-office.
Flexible schedule; fit around classes. Time-zone friction if there’s little overlap.
Build async communication and documentation skills. Self-management required; easy to drift without structure.
Lower costs (no commute/housing). Company culture can feel distant without proactive effort.

How to apply & stand out (ATS + proof)

  • Mirror keywords: Use the posting’s exact phrases (e.g., “PivotTables,” “React,” “customer interviews”).
  • Top-third proof: Put a linkable mini-project at the top of your resume (dashboard, repo, portfolio).
  • Short cover letter: Hook (2 lines) → 3–5 keyword fit → 1 proof story → availability. See cover letter for internship.
  • Submit early: Many remote roles are rolling. First week = best odds.

FAQ



How many applications should I send for remote roles?


Plan 50–150 targeted submissions across the season. Prioritize rolling postings and apply within the first week.



What should my resume show for remote work?


Evidence of async success: clear writing, linked docs/dashboards/repos, and shipped outcomes with numbers.



What if I’ve never worked remotely?


Run a tiny project in public (GitHub, Figma, Looker, Notion). Document decisions and results. Link it in the top third of your resume.
